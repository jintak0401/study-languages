# 2026-06-08 · Relative Clauses: who / which / that

## Grammar point

**Relative clauses** make sentences richer by describing a noun with extra information.

| Pronoun | Used for | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **who** | people | the teacher **who** helped me |
| **which** | things | the book **which** I'm reading |
| **that** | people or things (informal) | the method **that** works best |

**Structure**: noun + relative pronoun + rest of the clause

### ✅ Key rules

1. The relative pronoun follows its noun directly.
2. Never use **'what'** as a relative pronoun.
3. Don't add an extra pronoun inside the clause.

| Wrong | Right |
|---|---|
| The book **what** I'm reading... | The book **that** I'm reading... |
| The person who **he** helped me | The person **who** helped me |

## New vocabulary

| Word | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **mention** | 언급하다 | She mentioned a book that really helped her. |
| **impress** | 인상을 주다 | Speaking naturally impresses people. |
| **specific** | 구체적인 | Can you give me a specific example? |
| **relate to** | 공감하다 | I relate to people who feel nervous about speaking. |
| **plenty of** | 많은, 충분한 | There are plenty of topics we can talk about. |

## New expressions

> **The thing that I find hardest about English is speaking without thinking.**  
> (영어에서 제가 가장 어렵게 느끼는 건 생각 없이 말하는 거예요.)

> **I'd love to find a conversation partner who speaks only in English.**  
> (영어로만 말하는 대화 상대를 찾고 싶어요.)

## Tips for conversation

- In everyday speech, **'that'** is the most flexible — it works for people and things.
- **'Who'** sounds slightly more formal but is always correct for people.
- Relative clauses let you be more **specific** without needing two separate sentences.
